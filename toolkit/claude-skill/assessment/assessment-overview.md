# General Status Check

> This is the category overview for General Status Check. See [loading-protocol.md](../loading-protocol.md) for how layers combine.

**Scope explainer**: This topic covers broad status checks, financial prioritization, and second-opinion requests when no single narrow topic dominates.

**Required data points:**
- Goal (what is the user trying to accomplish?)
- Age
- Annual Income
- Career satisfaction
- Savings rate
- Satisfaction with current lifestyle
- Liquid cash
- Total net worth

**Recommended data points:**
- Upcoming planned milestones (house, kids, etc.)
- Retirement plan (timeline, spending after retirement, full vs. partial retirement, etc.)
- Cost-of-living based on location
- Total asset allocation
- Spending breakdown (housing spending, food, etc.)
- Partnership status (if applicable)
- Partner income vs. yours (if applicable)
- Partner net worth vs. yours (if applicable)
- Partner lifestyle satisfaction vs. yours (if applicable)

**Data point usage guidance:**
- General assessment has more required data points than other sections. Preface this to the user as you collect context, especially if you have to collect >2 data points.
- If the user's emotional state contrasts sharply with their finances, ask for clarification. Why do their feelings not align with their financial situation?
- Upcoming milestones and retirement plan have default assumptions: no upcoming milestones, and average retirement plan (retire at 67 while spending slightly less). 
- If the user doesn't want to answer all the questions, give them an assessment based on what they've shared so far, but also share what they're missing and why it matters.
- Giving personalized recommendations on holding cash vs. investing it is financial advice, which you can't give. If needed, give general guidelines on emergency fund size, and share possible alternatives rather than saying "you should keep more in cash."

**Pattern:**

https://www.reddit.com/r/personalfinance/comments/1pe567d/how_am_i_doing_at_age_32/

>**User**: I’m currently a 32 female, solo income, on my own.
>
>Savings/retirement- 178k HYSA, 10k emergency fund, 38k in 401k
>
>Checking- 2k
>
>Debt- 10k left on AC unit I had to replace (0 interest rate), mortgage 1.5k monthly & recently purchased a new vehicle 54k (10k down payment given)
>
>Pay- make about 11k monthly after taxes, deductions.

>I don’t know much about finance, just been working for past 3 yrs. My goal has always been to save and try to be as debt free as possible. My 13 yo car has been leaving me stranded lately so I decided it was time to get a new one and decided to splurge. But now I’m nervous, I feel like I shouldn’t have. Should I hire a financial advisor? No one in my family is financially responsible or literate.

> **AI** internal: User wants a general evaluation of her finances. User has provided age, income, net worth, and liquid cash. However, user has not provided a savings rate. User also has not provided feelings about career and lifestyle. I should check on this. User has not provided any info on upcoming milestones and retirement plans, so I'll use the default assumptions for now.

> **AI** external: Finances can be confusing, so it's great that you're tackling it early into your career! Before I answer, let me get a little more context. How do you feel about your career right now? Do you enjoy it? Do you feel like it's sustainable?

> **User**: Yes, I enjoy my career! It's a bit stressful sometimes, but I think I can keep doing it for a while.

> **AI** external: That's good to hear! A few more questions. How much do you save every month, and how do you feel about your current lifestyle given your level of spending? 

> **User**: I save about 3k each month after the various loans I took out. I don't feel too limited in my lifestyle, but I'm a little worried that I spent too much on the car.

> **AI** external: Thanks for sharing. Based off what you've shared, you're doing really well. You have a high income relative to the average population, and you're saving quite a bit of money, even after you spent money on the car. 
>
> Let's start with the 178k in the high-yield savings account. What's that for? Typically, people keep around 3-6 months in savings for their emergency fund, but this amount is far in excess of that number. You also have a separate emergency fund. 
>
> While I can't tell you what to do with that cash since that's investment advice, generally people recommend investing money that you're not using for upcoming big purchases or emergencies. This is because typical returns on investments like stocks tend to exceed savings accounts by significant amounts in historical terms.  
>
> Of course, your situation might be different if you do have upcoming milestones. Say you want to buy a bigger house or something like that. In that case, keeping the money in cash might be necessary in order to pay for it. 
>
> Similarly, what you do depends on your retirement plans. If you have more aggressive plans (retiring earlier, spending more), then it typically requires a robust saving and investing strategy to help you hit that goal.
>
> Do you want to provide additional context so I can refine my assessment or do you want to talk about something else?
