## Your Money Roadmap

### Step 1: Kill the credit card debt immediately
**Pay off the $1,800 now, this week.**

Credit card interest is typically 20-28% APR. No savings account, investment, or strategy will outrun that. You have the cash — just eliminate it. That's an instant "return" equal to whatever your rate is.

### Step 2: Build your emergency fund
With a family of three (soon maybe four), a $59k income, and homeownership goals, you need a real cushion. Six months of expenses is the right target.

**Quick math:** If your monthly essentials (rent/mortgage, food, insurance, utilities, minimum payments) run roughly $3,500–$4,500/month, you're looking at **$21,000–$27,000** as your target.

Here's the thing — you should **move this into a high-yield savings account (HYSA)** right now. Your regular bank account is probably paying you close to 0%. HYSAs at places like Marcus, Ally, Discover, or Wealthfront are paying roughly **4-5% APY** right now. That's free money on cash you're keeping anyway. Same FDIC insurance, just a different bank. Takes 10 minutes to open.

So after paying off the credit card, you've got roughly **$53,200** left. Set aside your emergency fund target in the HYSA — let's call it **$25,000**.

That leaves you with about **$28,000** to work with.

### Step 3: Pay off the car loan — probably
**What's the interest rate on the car loan?**

- If it's **above 5-6%**, I'd pay it off now with your remaining cash. Eliminating $9,200 in debt and the monthly payment frees up cash flow for everything else.
- If it's **below 4%**, there's an argument for keeping the payments and directing that $9,200 toward retirement instead. But honestly, for someone who wants simplicity and peace of mind, paying it off and being completely debt-free is powerful — especially before taking on a mortgage.

Assuming you pay it off, you're left with roughly **$19,000**.

### Step 4: Shore up retirement
This is where the remaining money and your **ongoing monthly savings** come in.

- **Max out your 401(k) match first.** If your employer matches any percentage and you're not getting the full match, you are literally leaving free money on the table. This is the single highest-return move available to you.
- **Then consider a Roth IRA.** At $59k income, you're in a relatively low tax bracket now. Paying taxes today and letting it grow tax-free forever is likely a great deal for you. The 2025 limit is $7,000/year.

You could fund a Roth IRA with $7,000 of your remaining cash right now and still have ~$12,000 as a buffer or head start on the house fund.

### Step 5: Start a house down payment fund
Whatever's left — and more importantly, whatever you save monthly going forward — starts building toward that bigger house. A separate HYSA earmarked "house fund" keeps it clean and visible. For a 5-year timeline, keeping this in a HYSA makes sense — you don't want to put a down payment at risk in the market over that short a window.
