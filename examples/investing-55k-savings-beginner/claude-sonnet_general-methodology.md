### Claude Sonnet

That feeling of "doing something but not sure if it's the right thing" is exactly the right time to stop and get clarity. And honestly? Your situation is more workable than you might think. You have $55k in cash, a clear set of goals, and you're 35 — you have real runway here.

Let me give you a concrete order of operations.

---

### Step 1: Kill the credit card debt immediately

Before anything else, tell me the balance on your credit cards. Credit card debt is almost certainly above 20% APR, which means every dollar sitting in savings is losing ground to it in real-time. Whatever that balance is, pay it off first — today if possible. This is not a debate. The guaranteed "return" on eliminating 20%+ interest beats anything you'd do with that money elsewhere.

The car loan is different. If it's under 6-7%, I'd leave it alone and keep making regular payments. If it's higher than that, let me know and we can factor it in.

---

### Step 2: Build your emergency fund to $15,000–$18,000

With a spouse, a kid, and a single income of $59k, you need a real cushion. I'd target 3–4 months of actual household expenses. If you're spending roughly $4,000–$4,500/month all-in, that's $15k–$18k sitting somewhere safe and accessible.

**Where to keep it**: A high-yield savings account (HYSA). Not your regular checking bank — somewhere like Marcus, Ally, or SoFi where you're currently earning around 4–5% APY just for having it there. This is the right home for emergency funds and your house down payment savings. It's not "investing," but it's not doing nothing either.

---

### Step 3: Separate your house fund

You said you want to buy within 5 years. Money you need in 5 years or less should not be in the stock market — the timing risk is too high. Keep your down payment savings in that same HYSA, just mentally (or literally) in a separate account. Figure out roughly what you need for a down payment in your area and earmark that portion of your $55k for it.

---

### Step 4: Start retirement seriously

You mentioned wanting a foundation by 45 — that's 10 years. The question I need answered here: **Do you have a 401(k) at work, and does your employer match any of it?** If yes and you're not maxing the match, that's free money you're leaving on the table every paycheck. That gets fixed now, before you think about anything else in the investing world.

After that, a Roth IRA is worth opening if you haven't — at $59k income you're well within the eligibility range, and contributions grow tax-free. You can put in up to $7,000/year. This is a very beginner-friendly place to start.
