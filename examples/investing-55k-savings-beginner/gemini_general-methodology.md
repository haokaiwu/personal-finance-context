### Gemini

This gives me a fantastic picture, and first off: take a deep breath. Having $55,000 in cash while making $59,000 a year is a massive accomplishment. You essentially have almost a full year of your gross salary saved up. You are in a highly secure position to make these next moves.

Since financial uncertainty and unexpected expenses stress you out, we are going to prioritize peace of mind. Here is exactly how I would structure this money based on your goals:

**1. Nuke the credit card debt immediately.** 
Take money from the $55k and pay off the credit card balance in full today. Credit card debt usually has an interest rate of 20% or more. There is no investment in the world that will safely out-earn what that debt is costing you. Paying it off gives you an instant, guaranteed return and frees up your monthly cash flow. 

**2. Lock in your 6-month emergency fund.**
Since unexpected expenses cause you stress, calculate what you and your wife spend on bare-bones essentials in one month (housing, groceries, utilities, minimum debt payments). Multiply that by six. Take that exact amount out of the $55k and put it into a **High-Yield Savings Account (HYSA)**. A HYSA is just like a regular bank account, but it pays you around 4% to 5% in interest right now, rather than the 0.01% most traditional banks pay. This money will grow safely, but you can access it within a couple of days if an emergency hits. 

**3. Address the car loan.**
Whether you pay this off immediately with your savings or continue making monthly payments comes down to the interest rate. If the interest rate on the car loan is over 6% or 7%, I'd prioritize paying it off right now from the savings. If it's lower than 4% or 5%, you can likely just keep making your regular monthly payments and let your cash sit in that HYSA earning interest instead.

**4. Earmark the house down payment.**
You mentioned wanting to buy a house in less than five years. A strict rule of investing is that money needed in less than five years shouldn't be in the stock market—the market is too volatile in the short term, and you don't want a market dip to happen right when you need to buy a house. Keep the cash you need for the house in your HYSA alongside your emergency fund, or put it into a Certificate of Deposit (CD), which locks in a guaranteed interest rate for a set amount of time.

**5. Start building the retirement foundation.**
Whatever money is left over after paying off the credit card, securing the 6-month emergency fund, dealing with the car loan, and setting aside house money is what you can invest for retirement. A great starting point is opening a **Roth IRA** (Individual Retirement Account). You can contribute up to $7,000 this year. The money grows tax-free, and you won't pay taxes when you pull it out in retirement. Within that account, you'd look into low-cost, broadly diversified investments like "target-date funds" or "index funds" that spread your money across the whole stock market so you don't have to pick individual stocks.
