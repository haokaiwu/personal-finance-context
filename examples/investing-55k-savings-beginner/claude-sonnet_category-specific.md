## Here's How I'd Prioritize Your $55k

### Step 1: Wipe out the credit card — immediately ($1,800)
This is the only no-brainer in your whole situation. Credit card debt almost certainly costs you 20%+ in interest. Nothing else you do with that money will return 20%. Pay it off this week and don't look back. That leaves you with ~$53,200.

### Step 2: Build a real emergency fund (~$15,000–$18,000)
You're a family of three with a mortgage, a kid in daycare, and another potentially on the way. Your monthly expenses are $3,500–$4,000, which means you need **4–5 months of expenses** liquid and accessible — roughly $15,000–$18,000. This isn't money that "works hard," it's money that keeps you from going into debt when life happens (job loss, medical bill, car repair, etc.).

If you don't already have a **High-Yield Savings Account (HYSA)**, open one. Current rates are around 4–4.5% APY — it's not exciting, but it beats a standard savings account significantly, and your money stays accessible. Look at Marcus, Ally, or SoFi.

### Step 3: Attack the car loan ($9,200)
At this point you'd have roughly $35,000 left. You mentioned wanting to knock out the car loan in the next couple years — you could do it right now and be completely debt-free. The math depends on your interest rate (you didn't mention it), but if it's above 5–6%, paying it off early is probably worth it. If it's a low rate like 2–3%, it's less urgent and you might be better off keeping that cash for your other goals.

**What's the interest rate on the car loan?** That'll tell me whether to push you to pay it off now or let it ride.

### Step 4: Start funding retirement — now, not later ($5,000–$7,000/year)
You said you feel behind at 35 — you're not in crisis, but you're right that now is the time to build a foundation. Here's the priority order for retirement accounts:

1. **401(k) up to your employer match** — if your employer matches any portion, contribute at least that much. It's an instant 50–100% return on that money. Do you have a 401(k) and does your employer match?
2. **Roth IRA** — at $59k income, you're well within the income limits. You can contribute up to $7,000/year. The Roth is great for you because contributions (not earnings) can be withdrawn penalty-free if you ever need them, and growth is tax-free in retirement.

### Step 5: The house and second kid — save strategically
After your emergency fund and debt are sorted, whatever's left (~$20,000–$25,000 depending on the car) can start working toward a down payment or second baby costs. A HYSA or short-term CD ladder makes sense here since you need this money in ~5 years and can't afford to lose it in a market downturn.
