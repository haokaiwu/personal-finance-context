## Here's What I'd Do With Your $55K

### Step 1: Kill the Credit Card Debt — Today
**Pay off that $1,800 credit card balance immediately.** At 20% interest, this is the single best "investment" you can make. Every month you carry that balance, you're losing money faster than any savings account or investment could earn it. This is a no-brainer.

**Remaining cash: ~$53,200**

### Step 2: Pay Off the Car Loan
**Pay off the $9,200 car loan next.** At 6.4%, this is above the threshold where paying it off is clearly better than letting it sit. You wanted to pay this off within a couple years anyway — just do it now. You have the cash, and it frees up your monthly car payment for better uses going forward.

**Remaining cash: ~$44,000**

### Step 3: Build Your Emergency Fund
You mentioned wanting 6 months of expenses. With a toddler, a wife, and another kid potentially on the way, that's smart. Without knowing your exact monthly expenses, I'd estimate you need somewhere in the range of **$15,000–$20,000** set aside in a **high-yield savings account** (HYSAs are paying ~4-5% right now — way better than a regular bank account). This money stays liquid and boring on purpose — it's not for growing, it's for sleeping well at night.

**Remaining cash after emergency fund: ~$24,000–$29,000**

### Step 4: Start Your Down Payment Fund
You've got 5 years until you want a bigger house. Take the remaining cash and park it in a **high-yield savings account** (or a CD ladder if you know you won't need it for 2+ years). This is your down payment seed money. With $24K+ as a starting point and $400–500/month in savings, you could realistically have **$50,000+** saved in 5 years — and that's before your potential promotion.

### Step 5: Get Retirement Rolling
You mentioned wanting a solid retirement foundation by 45. Here's the thing — you're 35, and it doesn't sound like you have any retirement savings yet. That's not a disaster, but it does mean you need to start *now*. Here's how:

- **If your employer offers a 401(k) with a match** — contribute at least enough to get the full match. That's free money, and skipping it is like turning down a raise.
- **If no employer match (or after you've maxed the match)** — look into opening a **Roth IRA**. You're in a moderate income bracket where Roth contributions make a lot of sense: you pay taxes now while your rate is lower, and withdrawals in retirement are tax-free.

I know retirement feels less urgent than the house or the car, but the math is brutal if you wait. Every year you delay costs you significantly more to catch up later.
