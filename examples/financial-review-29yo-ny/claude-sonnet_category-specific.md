## On the Master's Degree: "Finishing the Sentence" Changes the Math

The way you described it — licensure as the ceiling, not a pivot — is actually the most financially defensible reason to pursue a graduate degree. You're not betting on a new field. You're unlocking the credential that gets you full access to the work you're already doing adjacently. That meaningfully lowers the risk.

Here's what the financial stress-test actually looks like for your situation:

**The cost side:**
LMHC/LCSW programs in New York range from roughly $30K at a public university (SUNY, CUNY systems) to $60K+ at private schools. This is the single most important variable. A $30K program on top of your existing $19K loans puts you at ~$49K total. A $60K program puts you at ~$79K. That's not a small difference — it's potentially the difference between manageable and burdensome for a counseling salary.

**The income side:**
Licensed counselors in New York start around $55–70K in nonprofit/agency settings, which isn't a dramatic jump from $47K. But the ceiling is different: private practice or specialized clinical roles can reach $80–100K+, and in New York specifically, that's realistic with 5–10 years of post-licensure experience. The "finishing the sentence" framing suggests you already know the environment — so you probably have a realistic read on those numbers.

**The real question is: can you stay employed during the program?**
Most LMHC programs are designed with working adults in mind — evening/weekend cohorts are common, especially in New York. If you can stay at 80–100% of your current income during the program, the financial hit is almost entirely the tuition cost, not a multi-year income gap. That changes the calculus dramatically. If you'd have to drop to part-time or stop working, the math gets harder.

**My read:** If you find a CUNY or SUNY program in the $25–35K range and can stay employed during it, this is very likely worth doing — both financially and personally. If you're looking at a $55K+ private program, I'd push back hard and ask what it offers that a public program doesn't.
