### Gemini

First, take a deep breath. Reading personal finance forums can absolutely make you feel like you're drastically behind, but when you look at your actual financial behaviors, you are doing incredibly well. 

You have zero credit card debt, you're contributing 15% to retirement, and you are saving almost 40% of your take-home pay ($1,000 out of ~$2,400). That savings rate is fantastic. Your net worth is hovering right around zero (roughly $20k in assets minus $19k in student loans), which at 29 means your primary bottleneck isn't your financial discipline—it's your income.

Here is direct guidance on how to prioritize your next moves based on your situation:

**1. The Car is an emergency—treat it like one.**
Because you drive for work, a reliable car isn't just a lifestyle upgrade; it's a requirement to keep your income flowing. If the Honda dies next week, use your HYSA to buy a newer, reliable used car in cash (or put a massive down payment on one). Do not feel guilty about draining the emergency fund for this. This is the exact emergency you saved that $10,236 for. The peace of mind and job security are worth more than looking at a high balance in your savings account.

**2. Put the "saving for a house" and "more into retirement" goals on hold.**
Your gut instinct to minimize the car's impact so you can start saving for a house and retirement is a good instinct for five years from now, but it's the wrong move today. You are facing three massive transitions: a dying car, a roommate shakeup, and a potential return to school. Cash is your best friend right now. Keep the 403b at 15%, but do not increase it. Do not start a house fund. Hoard your extra $1,000/month into your HYSA until the dust settles on the car and the degree.

**3. Be ruthlessly analytical about the Master's degree.**
You are absolutely right that you need to increase your income. However, a Master's in Counseling is a notoriously dangerous degree from a personal finance perspective. These programs can easily cost $40,000 to $80,000+, but entry-level counselors (even licensed ones) often start at $50,000 to $60,000 depending on where you are in New York. You need to run a strict Return on Investment (ROI) calculation. Do not take on another $50,000 in student loans just to bump your salary by $10,000. Look for the cheapest in-state or public university option, or heavily research alternative career pivots that don't require a master's to boost your income.

**4. Keep the Art Studio.**
At $250/mo, this is a core part of your community and mental health. Personal finance is about getting what you want out of life. Given your incredibly low rent ($600 is a steal in NY State), you can easily afford to keep this space. Don't cut the things that bring you joy when you're already operating this leanly.
