## Blind Spots and Honest Flags

### 1. The Student Loan Deferment Deserves Scrutiny
- **Deferment until 2028 is not the same as forgiveness** – interest behavior during deferment depends on your loan types
- If these are unsubsidized loans, **interest may be accruing silently**
- You should log into studentaid.gov and confirm exactly what's happening to that balance right now
- $19,000 is manageable but needs a repayment plan before 2028 arrives suddenly

### 2. The Master's Degree Question Is the Biggest Financial Decision Here
This deserves more weight than the car because the numbers are larger and less reversible

**Ask yourself specifically:**
- What is the actual salary ceiling in your counseling field with a master's vs. without?
- What will the program cost and what would new loan amounts look like stacked on $19k?
- Are there employer tuition assistance programs available to you now?
- Would an LMHC or similar licensure genuinely open significantly higher-paying roles in your area?
- Could you take one course while working to test your commitment before fully enrolling?

The counseling field has **real licensure-to-income pathways** but also genuine salary ceilings depending on setting. Research actual job postings with salaries in your target role before committing.

### 3. No IRA Is a Gap Worth Closing
- You have no Roth IRA, which is a missed opportunity especially at your income level
- At $47,861 gross you're in a range where **Roth contributions make strong sense** – you're likely in a lower bracket now than you will be later
- Even $50–100/month into a Roth IRA builds the habit and the account history
- The 403b is good but an IRA gives you more investment flexibility and control

### 4. The $250 Studio Is Worth Examining Honestly
This isn't a judgment on its value to you – community and workspace matter. But at your income and savings rate it represents **~10% of your take-home**, which is significant. Questions worth sitting with:
- Is there any path where the studio generates income that offsets the cost?
- If the car situation or master's degree creates financial pressure, is this the first thing that moves?
- Having already decided it's a high priority is fine, just make sure that decision is conscious and revisited periodically

### 5. Mileage Reimbursement Reliability
You noted net pay includes an averaged mileage payment. A few things to verify:
- Is this reimbursement reliable and consistent, or does it vary significantly?
- If you change vehicles your actual gas and maintenance costs will shift
- Make sure you're tracking actual mileage carefully for tax purposes if any portion is tax-relevant

## The Car Decision

This is the most immediate practical question. Some framing:

**If the car is repairable for under ~$1,500–2,000:**
Strongly consider keeping it. You'd preserve your emergency fund cushion and buying runway.

**If you need to replace it:**
- Target something in the **$8,000–13,000 range** with a known reliability record (Civic, Corolla, Camry, Fit, etc.)
- Avoid financing if possible, or keep any loan **short term and low rate**
- A car payment right now competes directly with your IRA startup, emergency fund top-up, and master's savings
- Do not stretch for a newer or nicer car than you need – it's a work tool

**Your gut is right:** minimize the car's financial impact as much as possible.

---

## Priority Stack Recommendation

Given everything, here's a suggested order of operations:

1. **Resolve the car situation** with minimum financial damage
2. **Clarify student loan interest status** – this week, not eventually
3. **Open a Roth IRA** even with small contributions – Fidelity or Vanguard, total market index fund, set and forget
4. **Rebuild/reach $15k emergency fund** – your instinct here is correct especially given potential tuition and car overlap
5. **Research the master's program concretely** before next year – costs, outcomes, employer assistance options
6. **Revisit 403b percentage** once IRA is open – 15% is solid but make sure you're getting any full employer match first

---

## Bottom Line

You're not behind in a crisis sense. You're behind in the sense that the next 2–3 years have several large decisions arriving simultaneously – car, possible tuition, roommate change, housing costs – and they interact with each other. The work right now is **sequencing and information gathering**, not panic.

Your savings instinct and debt avoidance habits are genuinely strong foundations. The main risk is making one large decision (especially the degree) without fully modeling how it interacts with the others.
