## Where I'd Focus — In Priority Order

### 1. The Car: Keep It Cheap and Simple

Your instinct here is right. You drive for work, so reliability isn't optional — but this is exactly the kind of situation where people accidentally blow up their finances. Here's my take:

- **If the Honda passes inspection/repair for under ~$1,000–1,500**, keep driving it. A 2006 Honda with known issues is still better than a car payment you don't need.
- **If it's toast**, buy the cheapest reliable used car you can find — ideally $8,000–12,000 range, paid in cash or with the smallest loan possible. Think Civic, Corolla, Mazda3 — boring and bulletproof.
- **Do not finance more than you need to.** Every dollar of car payment competes with your emergency fund rebuild, your potential grad school savings, and your breathing room.

Your emergency fund at $10K is solid for your current expenses (~4+ months), but a car purchase could gut it. If you do have to buy, I'd pause extra retirement contributions temporarily (drop from 15% to whatever your employer match is) and aggressively rebuild the emergency fund back to $10-15K. That's a short-term move for a specific purpose — not a retreat from good habits.
