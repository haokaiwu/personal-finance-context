### Gemini

First off, take a deep breath. It is incredibly common to look at personal finance forums (like the "Prime Directive" on Reddit) and feel like you are drastically behind. 

By the strict traditional metric of "have 1x your salary saved for retirement by age 30," yes, you are a bit behind. **However, context is everything.** You have absolutely no high-interest debt, your student loans are deferred, you have a fully funded emergency fund, and you are living well below your means. Most importantly, **you are contributing 15% to retirement.** On a salary of $48k, saving that much of your income requires immense discipline. You have built excellent financial muscles. 

Here is a review of your situation, your blind spots, and how to navigate the upcoming hurdles.

### 1. The Immediate Hurdle: The Car
Because you drive for work, a reliable car is not a luxury; it is your livelihood. 
*   **The Blind Spot:** If you buy a newer used car, **your car insurance will almost certainly go up.** A financed car requires comprehensive and collision coverage. Budget for your $85/mo insurance to potentially double. 
*   **The Strategy:** Do not drain your $10k Emergency Fund to zero to buy a car in cash. You need a cash buffer for your upcoming roommate change and potential grad school. If the Honda dies, aim to put $3,000–$5,000 down on a sensible used car (Toyota/Honda/Mazda) and finance the rest. Shop around at local credit unions *before* you go to a dealership to get a pre-approved auto loan with a lower interest rate. 

### 2. The Medium-Term Hurdle: Housing & Master's Degree
*   **Housing:** Take the $600 room if you can manage it. Saving that $50/month ($600/year) will help offset the new $50/month WiFi bill you're taking on. Ensure you get your backup roommate locked in as soon as possible so you aren't left holding the bag for the whole rent. 
*   **The Master's Degree (Counseling):** You are entirely correct that your current income ceiling is a bit low for NY State. Going back to school to raise your earning potential is a great idea, but **this is your biggest financial blind spot.** 
    *   Counseling degrees often require hundreds of hours of *unpaid* practicum/internships. Many students find it impossible to work full-time while doing this.
    *   Before you enroll, look at the ROI (Return on Investment). What does a first-year counselor make in your part of NY? ($60k? $70k?) Compare that to how much debt you will take on. Look heavily into affordable CUNY/SUNY state programs rather than expensive private universities.

### 3. Your Budget & Savings
Your living expenses are incredibly lean. Keeping your total rent under $650 in NY State is a massive win. 
*   **The Art Studio ($250/mo):** Strictly speaking, this is a luxury that takes up 10% of your take-home pay. *However*, because your rent is so incredibly low, you can absolutely afford this. Community and mental health are priceless. Keep it.
*   **Subscriptions ($94/mo + $548/yr):** This equates to roughly $140 a month in subscriptions. This is a bit heavy for your income level. It might be worth doing an audit here and canceling 2 or 3 things you rarely use to free up an extra $30–$40 a month.
*   **Emergency Fund:** You currently have roughly 6 months of basic living expenses saved ($10k). This is perfect. If you have to use some of it for a car down payment, your goal of building it back up to $15k is incredibly smart, especially heading into grad school. 

### 4. Long-Term: Retirement & Buying a House
*   **Retirement:** Keep that 403(b) at 15%. Because you started a little late, keeping this contribution rate high is how you will catch up. You don't necessarily need an IRA right now; keep it simple and just let the 403(b) grow. 
*   **Buying a House:** Pause this goal. You cannot effectively save for a house, buy a newer car, pay for a Master's degree, and survive an internship period all at the same time on $48k a year. Put the house goal on a 5-to-7-year horizon. Focus on the car, the degree, and increasing your income first. 

### Summary Action Plan:
1.  **Prepare for the car:** Check your credit score today, and look at auto loan rates at local credit unions so you are ready if the Honda dies next week. Expect your insurance to go up.
2.  **Lock in the roommate:** Formalize the agreement with your backup roommate to protect your exceptionally low housing costs.
3.  **Research the Master's strictly by the numbers:** Calculate the exact cost of the degree, the reality of the unpaid internship hours, and the starting salary of a counselor in your area. 
4.  **Stay the course:** Keep up the 15% retirement contribution, and keep pushing your leftover cash into your HYSA. 

You are doing a fantastic job managing your money. You have a great foundation; now it's just about carefully navigating these next few life transitions to push your income higher.
