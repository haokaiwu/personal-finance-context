## The Car Situation

Your instinct is right — minimize the impact. Here's how I'd frame it:

**If the Honda survives inspection**: Keep driving it. Every month that car runs is a month you're building savings instead of making payments. A 2006 Honda that's been maintained can surprise you.

**If it doesn't**: You need reliable transportation for work, and the mileage reimbursement makes this partly income-protecting. I'd target a $8,000–12,000 used car (think 2016–2019 Civic, Corolla, or Mazda3). Here's the tension:

- Your emergency fund is $10,236. Pulling from it for a car is exactly what it's for — this is a legitimate emergency/major expected expense.
- But draining it below $5,000 would leave you thin, especially with the roommate transition happening simultaneously.

**My recommendation**: If you need a car, spend $8,000–10,000 max. Pay cash from the emergency fund rather than taking on a car loan. Yes, your emergency fund drops to ~$2,000, which is uncomfortable. But the alternative — a car payment of $250–350/mo for years — permanently reduces your savings capacity and delays everything else. Rebuild the emergency fund over the next several months at $600–700/mo and you're back to $5,000+ within about 5 months.

If the car costs more than $10k and you'd need financing, put down whatever keeps your emergency fund at $3,000 and finance the rest — but only if you can get a rate under 6%.

## Where Your Blind Spots Are

**1. The 0% APR loan — what is it and when does the rate change?** If it's a promotional rate that jumps to 20%+, you need a payoff plan before that date. How much is left on it?

**2. Your 403b is strong at 15%, but check the match.** Are you getting a full employer match? If the match maxes out at, say, 6%, and you're contributing 15%, you might temporarily reduce to the match percentage while you're navigating the car + roommate transition + potential grad school. That could free up $150–200/mo. I'd only do this short-term and bump it back up once things stabilize.

**3. The student loans in deferment — are they accruing interest?** Federal loans in deferment sometimes still accrue interest. If they're subsidized, you're fine. If unsubsidized, interest is quietly growing that $19,000. Worth confirming.

**4. The mileage reimbursement deserves scrutiny.** You said your net includes "an averaged mileage payment." Is that reimbursement actually covering your vehicle costs (depreciation, maintenance, gas), or is your car effectively subsidizing your job? If you're driving a lot for work, this matters — especially when pricing out a replacement car and its insurance.

**5. You didn't mention health insurance or any other benefits.** I'm assuming that's handled through work, but worth confirming nothing is falling through the cracks.

## The Master's Degree Question

This is the biggest financial decision on your horizon and it deserves real thought. A few things to consider:

- A master's in counseling typically costs $30,000–60,000+ depending on program. That would roughly double your student debt.
- Counselors in New York can earn $50,000–75,000+ depending on licensure and setting, but the jump isn't immediate — there's a supervised hours period post-degree.
- You'd likely lose income during the program (reduced work hours or full stop).

This isn't a reason not to do it — career fulfillment matters, and "I don't feel I'm making enough" is a valid signal. But the financial play is to **get your foundation solid before you go**: emergency fund rebuilt, car situation resolved, and ideally a dedicated "grad school fund" started. Starting a program next year is ambitious given everything else in motion. 18–24 months out might be more realistic financially.

**Before committing, I'd research**: total program cost for 2–3 specific schools you'd attend, whether you'd work during, expected post-degree income in your area, and whether your employer offers any tuition assistance.
