### Claude Sonnet

You've clearly thought this through carefully, and the picture is better than you might think for 29 in New York. Let me give you the honest assessment you asked for.

## The Good News First

**You're not behind — you're actually in a solid position.** No credit card debt, a real emergency fund, active retirement contributions, and $1,000/month in savings on a $47k salary in New York? That's genuinely good. A lot of 29-year-olds in HCOL areas are running on fumes.

Your instincts are also correct across the board. The priorities you named — minimize car impact, build emergency fund, more retirement, eventual house — are the right ones in roughly the right order.

---

## The Car Situation

This is your most urgent financial decision, so let's think through it clearly.

**If the car is fixable for under ~$2,000:** Fix it. Every month you keep a paid-off car is a month you're not paying $300–500 in car payments. That's the single best thing you can do for your savings rate right now.

**If it needs more than that, or is truly unreliable:** Replace it, but be aggressive about keeping the cost down. For a used car in the $8–12k range, you could pay cash or put a large down payment down and keep monthly payments under $150. Do not let a dealer talk you into a $20k car on a $47k salary — that's where this goes wrong for people.

**Key constraint:** You drive for work. Reliability isn't optional. Don't let the emergency fund goal make you keep an unsafe car. The $15k target is important, but not more important than your income source.

---

## Emergency Fund

Your $10,236 is good, and $15k is the right target. At your current savings rate you're a few months away even with increased expenses. Hit it before you redirect money anywhere else — especially with the car uncertainty and roommate transition happening simultaneously.

Once you're at $15k, that fund is done. Don't keep growing it indefinitely.

---

## Retirement — This Is Your Biggest Blind Spot

15% into your 403b on $47k is $7,179/year. That sounds strong, and it is — but I want to flag something:

**You have no Roth IRA and no Roth contributions.** At $47k gross, you're in a relatively low tax bracket right now. If you go back for your master's and your income rises significantly, you'll wish you'd locked in Roth contributions at today's rates. A Roth IRA also has more flexibility than a 403b — you can withdraw *contributions* (not earnings) penalty-free, which matters if a house purchase is on the horizon.

The question worth sitting with: **Is your 403b traditional or Roth?** If it's traditional, I'd seriously consider opening a Roth IRA and redirecting some of your 403b contributions there, especially before income potentially increases.

You're eligible to contribute up to $7,000/year to a Roth IRA at your income. Even $100–150/month gets you in the habit and takes advantage of your current tax situation.

## The Master's Degree

This one deserves more scrutiny than it usually gets. Counseling is meaningful work, but the ROI varies a lot depending on:
- What sector you plan to work in (school counseling vs. private practice vs. nonprofit)
- Whether you'd take on more federal loans or have employer support
- What realistic salary growth looks like

You're already in deferment on $19k. Adding a master's degree typically means another $30–60k in loans for a counseling program, and counseling salaries often start in the $45–55k range — not dramatically more than you're making now, especially in NY.

This isn't a reason not to do it. But I'd want you to stress-test the numbers before committing, especially if you're hoping to buy a house in the next 5 years. Those two goals can conflict badly.

## One Thing I'm Missing

You mentioned mileage reimbursement is averaged into your net — but I don't know if your employer fully covers your driving costs or if you're absorbing some of it. If you're putting real miles on a personal car for work and not being fully reimbursed, that's a hidden expense that could be significant, especially with a new car purchase coming.

Worth calculating whether your actual per-mile reimbursement rate covers wear, depreciation, and gas. If it doesn't, that's a negotiation conversation worth having with your employer.
