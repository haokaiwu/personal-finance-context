# Personal Finance Optimization Plan

You're in a really solid position for 25, so let's make the most of it.

---

## Emergency Fund First

You mentioned an $18K target with ~$2,350/month surplus. **Park this in a high-yield savings account immediately** while you build it up.

Good HYSA options right now:
- Marcus (Goldman Sachs)
- Ally Bank
- Wealthfront Cash Account
- SoFi (sometimes runs promotional rates)

You're looking at roughly **4-5% APY** depending on the account, which beats any traditional savings account by a mile. You'll hit your $18K target in about **7-8 months** if you're disciplined.

> **Don't skip this step to invest.** Your wife's income being variable as a realtor makes this emergency fund even more critical, especially in the Southern California real estate market which can be cyclical.

---

## Credit Card Strategy

### Keep your old cards open
This is straightforward. **Do not cancel them.** Your 780+ scores benefit from:
- **Average age of accounts** (closing old cards hurts this)
- **Credit utilization ratio** (more available credit = lower utilization percentage)
- **Number of accounts** (contributes to credit mix)

Just put a small recurring charge on each old card (like a streaming subscription) and set it to autopay. That keeps them active without any effort.

### Consolidate daily spending onto one strong rewards card
This makes total sense. You're spending ~$3K/month, which is $36K/year in spending power. That's meaningful reward potential you're leaving on the table with basic cards.

**Chase Sapphire Preferred** is worth considering because:
- $95 annual fee (easily offset by the rewards)
- 3x on dining, 2x on travel, various streaming/online grocery bonuses
- Points transfer to airline and hotel partners at strong value
- Sign-up bonus alone can be worth $600-900+

**Chase Sapphire Reserve** might be harder to justify right now at the $550 annual fee unless you travel frequently.

**Alternative to consider:** If most of your $3K is groceries, gas, and everyday stuff rather than dining and travel, look at something like the **Citi Double Cash (2% flat on everything)** or **Wells Fargo Active Cash (2% flat)**. Simpler, no annual fee, and no category tracking.

### The real question is your spending breakdown
Ask yourself where your $3K actually goes:

| If heavy on... | Best fit |
|---|---|
| Dining + travel | Chase Sapphire Preferred |
| Groceries | Amex Blue Cash Preferred |
| Everything spread out | 2% flat cash back card |
| Gas + groceries + streaming | Discover/category rotators you already have |

You can also run **two cards** strategically: one category card for your biggest spend area and one flat 2% card for everything else.

## After the Emergency Fund: Roth IRA

Once you hit $18K in savings, **immediately redirect that $2,350/month surplus into a Roth IRA.** Here's why this is almost a no-brainer for you:

### Why Roth at 25 is powerful
- You're likely in a **lower tax bracket now** than you will be later in your career
- Contributions grow **completely tax-free**
- Withdrawals in retirement are **tax-free**
- You can withdraw **contributions (not gains) anytime** without penalty, which acts as a secondary emergency layer
- **Decades of compound growth** ahead of you

### The numbers matter here
The 2024/2025 max is **$7,000/year** (~$583/month). That leaves you with roughly **$1,767/month still leftover** after maxing it.

### Where to open it
- **Fidelity, Schwab, or Vanguard** are all excellent choices
- Low-cost index funds are your friend at this age
- A simple **target-date fund** or a **total market index fund (like VTI or FXAIX)** is perfectly fine to start

### What to do with the remaining ~$1,767/month after Roth is maxed
Priority order:
1. **Does your employer offer a 401(k) with match?** Max the match if you haven't already (this is free money)
2. **Taxable brokerage account** for additional investing beyond tax-advantaged limits
3. **Save toward specific goals** (house down payment, car replacement fund, etc.)
