## My direct take

You’re in a solid position. You’re 25, cash-flow positive, no credit card debt, low fixed expenses relative to income, and you already know your priorities. That’s better than most people your age.

### 1) Finish the emergency fund first
Since your stated priority is an **$18k emergency fund within a year**, I’d do that first and keep it in a **HYSA**.

With about **$2,350 left over each month**, you can hit that pretty quickly unless your current cash is much lower than it sounds. Since you said a decent chunk of your $22k net worth is already cash, you may be closer than you think.

For your situation, the emergency fund matters more than squeezing out credit card rewards or rushing into a Roth IRA this second. You’re in Southern California, your future goal includes a house, and you’re relying on one income for core expenses. That makes liquidity valuable.

### 2) After the emergency fund, move hard into retirement
Once the emergency fund is fully set, I’d shift the monthly surplus toward **retirement savings**, starting with a **Roth IRA** if you’re eligible and not already capturing a strong workplace match elsewhere.

At 25, time is your biggest advantage. The biggest mistake here would be staying in “cash-building mode” for too long after the emergency fund is already done.

So the handoff should be clean:

- **Until EF is full** → HYSA
- **After EF is full** → Roth IRA / retirement contributions
- **After retirement contributions are on track** → extra cash toward house fund

### 3) Keep the house fund separate from the emergency fund
Do **not** mix your future home down payment money with your emergency fund.

Those are two different jobs:

- **Emergency fund** = protection
- **House fund** = planned spending

Once the emergency fund is done and retirement contributions are started, open a separate HYSA bucket for the future down payment. Since your time horizon is **3–5 years**, keeping house money in cash-style accounts is reasonable.

### 4) Credit cards: keep old cards open, use one main card
For the cards:

- **Keep your older no-annual-fee cards open**
- Put a small recurring charge on them occasionally if needed
- Use **one main card** for most spending if that makes your system easier

That’s the cleanest setup.

You do **not** need to consolidate to one card for credit-score reasons, but consolidating for simplicity is smart if it helps you stay organized.
