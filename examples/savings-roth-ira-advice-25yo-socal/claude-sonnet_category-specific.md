## 💳 Credit Card Strategy

**Keep both existing cards open. Do not cancel them.** Credit age is a real factor in your score, and closing old accounts shortens your average credit history. There's no cost to keeping them open — just use them occasionally so they don't get auto-closed.

On Chase Sapphire: if you're spending ~$3k/month and paying it off monthly (which you should be), a rewards card like Chase Sapphire Preferred or Reserve can genuinely return meaningful value — especially for travel, dining, and everyday purchases. Whether it's worth it depends on your actual spending patterns. If a chunk of your $3k goes toward travel, dining, or groceries, the Sapphire Preferred (moderate annual fee) or Reserve (higher fee, higher rewards) can easily pay for itself. If most of your spending is utilities and rent, the math gets murkier.

Consolidating spending onto one card for simplicity is totally fine — just make sure it's the card that earns the best rewards for *your* spending categories.

## Why a Roth IRA Is a Clear Win for You Right Now

At 25, this is one of the best financial moves you can make — and the reasoning is simple:

**Tax-free compounding for 35–40 years is a massive advantage.** Money you put into a Roth IRA grows completely tax-free. When you pull it out in retirement, you owe nothing — not on the contributions, not on the growth. At your age, even modest contributions can grow into significant tax-free wealth by retirement.

**You're likely in a lower tax bracket now than you will be later.** Roth accounts make the most sense when your current tax rate is lower than your expected future rate. At 25, earning $5,350/month (~$64k gross), you're almost certainly paying less in taxes now than you will be at peak earning years. Locking in today's rate is smart.

**The 2025 limit is $7,000/year ($583/month)** — very manageable with your $2,350 surplus once the emergency fund is done.
