## Credit Card Strategy

**Don't cancel your older cards.** Credit age is one of the most valuable things you can have, and it costs you nothing to keep them open. Use each one for a small recurring charge (Netflix, a subscription, whatever) so they stay active. Canceling them would hurt your credit score for years with zero upside.

**On Chase Sapphire** — yes, get it. You're in Southern California, presumably spending on dining, travel, or both, and the Sapphire Preferred earns 3x on dining and 2x on travel with meaningful transfer partners (United, Hyatt, etc.). The $95 annual fee pays for itself quickly if you travel even occasionally. If you and your wife take one trip a year, the points alone likely cover the fee multiple times over.

**Consolidate your day-to-day spending onto the Sapphire**, keep the older cards open with light usage, and pay everything in full monthly. That's the move.

## Emergency Fund vs. Roth IRA

You're targeting $18k for your emergency fund. The question is: where are you *now*?

- **If you're well under $18k in liquid savings**, prioritize filling the emergency fund first. At $2,350/month going in, you could hit $18k in under 8 months. Put it in a high-yield savings account (Ally, Marcus, SoFi — currently 4–5% APY). Once you hit the target, redirect that $2,350 to your Roth.
- **If you're close to $18k already**, split the surplus — keep a small monthly contribution flowing to savings to hit the target, and open the Roth IRA now to start the clock on tax-free growth. At 25, every year you delay the Roth costs you compounding years you can never get back.

**The Roth is 100% worth prioritizing once your emergency fund is covered.** The 2025 contribution limit is $7,000/year (~$583/month). With $2,350 in monthly surplus, you can max it and still have $1,700+ left to invest, save toward a house, or build longer-term wealth.
