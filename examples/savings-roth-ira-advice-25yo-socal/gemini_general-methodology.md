### Gemini

This is a fantastic position to be in at 25. Having $2,350 left over each month gives you a lot of leverage, and covering a Southern California lifestyle on a single income while your wife builds her real estate business is impressive. 

Based on what you’ve shared, here is exactly how I would optimize your next steps:

### 1. The Credit Card Strategy
**Do not cancel your older credit cards.** Closing them will lower your total available credit and eventually reduce your average age of accounts, which can drag down your 780+ credit score. Put a small, recurring subscription (like Netflix or Spotify) on them and set them to autopay, then put them in a drawer. 

**Go ahead and get a premium rewards card** like the Chase Sapphire Preferred, assuming you like to travel. Consolidating your $3,000/month expenses onto one card is smart because it simplifies your finances and guarantees you'll easily hit the sign-up bonuses that make these cards worth it. *The golden rule applies:* Only do this if you treat the credit card like a debit card and pay the statement balance in full every single month.

### 2. What to do with the $2,350/month
**Prioritize hitting your $18,000 emergency fund in a High-Yield Savings Account (HYSA) before investing in the Roth IRA.** 

Because you are essentially operating as a single-income household for expenses, your risk is slightly elevated if you were to lose your job. You need that 6-month cash buffer. At your current savings rate, even if you were starting from zero, you'd hit that $18k goal in under 8 months. Put the cash in an HYSA earning 4-5% so it's growing safely while remaining completely liquid.

**Once the $18,000 is fully funded, pivot aggressively to the Roth IRA.** Maxing it out currently requires $7,000 a year, which is less than $600 a month. With your excess cash flow, you'll be able to max yours out easily and still have plenty left over to either invest in a taxable brokerage account, save for a house down payment, or start maxing out a Roth IRA for your wife. 

### The Caveat
My advice above assumes you are starting from zero on the emergency fund and that you are completely debt-free. If you have any high-interest debt (anything over 8% APR, like carried credit card balances or high-rate personal loans), that completely changes the math. I would prioritize paying off high-interest debt before aggressively funding the savings account or the Roth IRA.
