Here is a breakdown of how you can optimize your finances, credit, and that $2,350/month surplus.

### 1. The $2,350 Surplus: HYSA vs. Roth IRA
You actually don't have to choose between the two—you make enough surplus to do **both**. Here is the recommended "Order of Operations" for your money:

*   **Step 1: Hit the Emergency Fund Goal (HYSA).** 
    Your $18,000 goal is perfect. Every dollar of that emergency fund should be sitting in a High-Yield Savings Account (HYSA). Right now, HYSAs are yielding around 4.25% to 5.00% (check out Ally, Marcus, SoFi, or Wealthfront). This means your fully funded $18k emergency fund will generate about $75+ a month in completely passive, risk-free income. 
*   **Step 2: Fund the Roth IRAs.**
    Since you are 25, **time is your greatest asset.** Money invested now will double roughly every 7-10 years in the stock market. The maximum contribution for a Roth IRA in 2024/2025 is $7,000 per person. 
    *   *The Math:* You can fund a Roth IRA for *both* you and your wife (a "Spousal IRA" allows a working spouse to fund a non-working spouse's IRA, though she likely qualifies on her own anyway). That’s $14,000 total per year, or roughly **$1,166/month**. 
*   **The Strategy:** Take your $2,350 surplus. Put $1,166 into your two Roth IRAs (invested in broad market index funds like S&P 500 or Total Stock Market). Put the remaining **$1,184/month** into your HYSA until you hit your $18k goal. Once the $18k is hit, you can start putting that extra $1,184 toward a future house down payment, a brokerage account, or vacations.

### 2. Credit Card Strategy: Consolidate or Upgrade?
Since you have 780+ credit scores, you are leaving money on the table by only using basic cards, assuming you pay your statement balance in full every month (which it sounds like you do).

*   **Should you get a premium card?** Yes. A card like the **Chase Sapphire Preferred (CSP)** or **Capital One Venture Rewards** is a great move. 
    *   The CSP has a $95 annual fee, but it usually comes with a massive sign-up bonus (often worth $600–$750+ in travel). If you put all your daily spending (groceries, gas, dining, utilities) on this card, you will rack up points quickly.
    *   *Note:* Ensure your normal organic spending allows you to hit the "Minimum Spend" for the sign-up bonus (usually $4,000 in the first 3 months) without buying things you don't need.
*   **What about Rent/Mortgage?** If a large chunk of your $3,000 expenses is rent, look into the **Bilt Mastercard**. It allows you to pay rent with no transaction fees and earn 1 point per dollar. It is currently the best card on the market for renters.

### 3. What to do with your old credit cards?
**Do NOT cancel them.** 
Part of your 780+ credit score is calculated based on two things:
1.  **Average Age of Accounts:** Older is better. Canceling your oldest cards will eventually shorten your credit history.
2.  **Credit Utilization:** This is how much credit you use vs. how much you have available. Having those old cards open increases your total available credit, which keeps your utilization percentage incredibly low.

*   *What to do:* As long as these older cards do not have an Annual Fee, keep them open. Put a tiny recurring charge on them (like Spotify or Netflix), set them to auto-pay the full balance each month, and throw the physical cards in a drawer. 

### Bonus: What to do with your wife's income?
Because your life is fully funded by your income, your wife’s realtor commissions act as a massive wealth-building lever. When she closes a deal, you can use that money to:
1.  **Reinvest in her business** (marketing, staging, etc.).
2.  **Turbo-charge investments.** As an independent contractor (1099), she has access to incredible retirement vehicles like a **Solo 401(k)** or **SEP IRA**, which allow her to shelter massive amounts of her commission from taxes. Strongly consider chatting with a CPA about how to optimize her real estate income for tax savings.

**Summary Action Plan:**
1. Open a HYSA and direct your EF savings there.
2. Open two Roth IRAs, automate contributions, and invest the funds in low-cost index funds.
3. Keep your old, no-fee credit cards open but put them in a drawer.
4. Apply for a great travel rewards card (like Chase Sapphire Preferred) and run all your normal, budgeted expenses through it, paying it off in full on the 1st of every month.
